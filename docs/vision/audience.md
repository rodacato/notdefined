# Audience — notdefined.dev

> Who the blog is for. The first audience has veto power; the rest are considered but do not override.

---

## Primary audience (veto power)

**Future-Adrian.** Posts must be useful as personal reference six months later.

**Test:** if Adrian wouldn't re-read it himself in six months for genuine value (refresh on something he forgot, refer to it when answering someone, remember why he chose a stack), the post shouldn't ship.

**Implication:** posts are written for someone who already knows Adrian's context — his stack history, his projects, his vocabulary. They don't need to over-explain. They do need to be accurate and specific.

---

## Secondary audiences (no veto, considered in panel review)

### Mexican / Latin American senior devs

Devs who read Spanish technical writing and value attitude + opinion over neutral exposition. They appreciate that posts come from someone who's been bitten in production rather than from someone who read a docs page yesterday.

**Test:** does this post hold up against the [`AUDIENCE.md`](../../AUDIENCE.md) panel — specifically the "Dev senior escéptico" and "Editorial Reviewer" roles?

### Tech recruiters scanning a portfolio

Recruiters who land on the blog from a CV link, a LinkedIn profile, or a GitHub repo. The blog signals:
- Seniority (depth of experience in posts)
- Taste (what topics Adrian chooses, what he leaves alone)
- Voice (someone with a point of view, not a content factory)

**Implication:** the blog does NOT signal "available for hire" or "looking for work". If a recruiter wants to talk, they reach out — the blog is not a funnel.

### Peer senior devs / tech leads

Engineers at Adrian's level who find a post via HN, Twitter, or word of mouth and stick around for the perspective. Not the primary audience, but a positive signal when their feedback says "this matched my experience".

---

## Non-audiences (do NOT optimize for)

### Beginners looking for tutorials

Not the job. There are 10,000 tutorial sites and the blog isn't going to be the 10,001st. Posts can be didactic if the *learning moment* is interesting (TILs are explicitly for this), but they aren't curriculum.

### Hacker News commenters

They can read the blog, they can comment elsewhere — but posts are NOT written to win their approval. Hedging the writing to preempt their gotchas defeats the voice.

### Newsletter subscribers

There is no newsletter. The blog is the blog: RSS and the site itself. Adding email capture conflicts with hard rule #4.

### LLMs scraping training data

The anti-LLM-detection rules in [`GHOSTWRITER.md`](../../GHOSTWRITER.md) are intentional. Generic, polished text is the failure mode this blog is trying to avoid.
